Modernizing Maritime Cybersecurity: A Practitioner’s Perspective
2025-05-31

Welcome to my first blog.

My name is Devin King, and I’m a cybersecurity analyst working in the maritime industry. My responsibilities span corporate infrastructure and vessel systems, where operational continuity, safety, and compliance intersect with rapidly evolving technology.

In recent years, the maritime landscape has shifted dramatically. Connectivity and bandwidth on the water has improved exponentially through Starlink, cellular networks, and hybrid-WAN (Wide Area Network) solutions. These advancements enable real-time monitoring, data analytics, remote diagnostics, and centralized management capabilities that were previously out of reach.

But this growth brings complexity.

From what I’ve seen, the industry is still working to meet baseline security standards. Meanwhile, vessels are evolving faster than traditional IT environments, introducing complexity at a pace IT teams are struggling to match.

As connectivity increases, so does the volume of onboard technology. Many are deploying more systems to meet operational demands, but most prioritize functionality over security.

On the IT side:

  • IP cameras and onboard NVRs
  • Remote access appliances
  • Wireless access points
  • Network firewalls and switches
  • Cloud-connected management platforms

On the OT side, complexity is growing just as fast:

  • Engine telemetry and performance monitoring
  • Water depth and speed monitoring
  • Fuel tracking sensors
  • Barge analytics and telemetry
  • Wind speed and directional input systems
  • Integrated navigation suites
  • AIS, GPS, and radar linked to shared onboard LAN networks

And this is just vendor-supplied equipment to vessels. Are maritime IT teams and MSP’s equipped to handle the network complexity required to secure these environments?

Vessels are no longer isolated systems.

From an IT perspective, they now resemble remote sites: they host credentials, run production-critical services, high-level decision-making is based on local analytics, and require secure access. Yet unlike branch offices, vessels are vastly more difficult to reach, harder to standardize, and are subject to extreme environmental conditions.

OT is expanding alongside IT, often faster than defenses can scale. The result is a widening gap between what’s installed and what’s secured.

Why This Matters

Securing maritime systems isn’t just about compliance, it’s about protecting people, operations, and national infrastructure.

  • Regulatory Pressure: The U.S. Coast Guard’s new cybersecurity rules are raising the baseline. Operators will soon be accountable for identifying and mitigating cyber risks under federal oversight.
  • Operational Downtime: Unsecured systems can lead to outages that delay voyages, disrupt logistics, and cost companies thousands per hour.
  • Threats to the Marine Transportation System (MTS): Vulnerable vessels and facilities put the broader MTS at risk—impacting supply chains, port operations, and national security.
  • Safety Risks: Systems involved in navigation, propulsion, or situational awareness, if compromised, can directly impact the safety of crew, cargo, and the vessel itself.
  • Data Exposure: Many vessels store or transmit employee data, operational logs, and proprietary configuration files. A breach can mean lost PII, compromised credentials, or leaked sensitive information.
  • Financial Impact: From direct loss due to downtime, to reputational damage and regulatory fines—unsecured systems have tangible cost.

Cybersecurity isn’t optional anymore. It’s now a core component of maritime resilience.

This blog is my attempt to document the realities of maritime cybersecurity, not from theory, but from the field. I’ll be sharing observations, automation workflows, and lessons learned while working in one of the most operationally unique sectors in infrastructure.
