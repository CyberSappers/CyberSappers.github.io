Navigating the IT/OT Divide
2025-06-08

In maritime environments, people often hear “IT/OT separation” tossed around like a buzzword. But many outside of cybersecurity don’t fully grasp what it means or why it matters so much. This isn’t just a technical preference. It’s a foundational safety requirement.

Understanding the Difference

  • IT (Information Technology) deals with email, internet, software, business systems — these are the digital tools used to manage operations and communication.

  • OT (Operational Technology) controls the ship itself — navigation, propulsion, engine systems, safety equipment, sensors.

They serve two very different purposes. One supports business. The other keeps the vessel operational and safe.

Why They Must Be Separated

  1. To Contain Risk If someone clicks a bad link in an email or plugs in a compromised USB on the IT side, it shouldn’t be able to affect navigation systems. Keeping these environments separate limits how far a problem can spread.

  2. To Prioritize Safety Business systems can go offline without immediate danger. OT systems can’t. If a safety control fails or a navigation system is tampered with, the consequences can be physical and immediate. The separation ensures business-side problems don’t put lives at risk.

  3. To Respect Different Needs IT changes often. OT changes slowly — often only during drydock or major service windows. They’re built with different priorities and update cycles. Putting them together creates unnecessary strain and potential instability.

  4. To Protect Against Mistakes Most cyber incidents come from human error, such as phishing. The more overlap between these two worlds, the easier it is for someone to accidentally cause a problem they didn’t understand, or provide a clear entry point for attackers. Separation provides a safety buffer.

What Awareness Looks Like

  • Recognize that networks on a vessel are very different. Some networks run business tools, others steer the ship or controls navigation.

  • Understand that convenience often comes at a cost. Easy file sharing or internet access from bridged systems can introduce unseen risks.

  • Support clear policies that restrict cross-connections unless absolutely necessary, and only with proper oversight.

  • Ask questions when new systems are added: Does this touch OT? Is there separation?

How It’s Done

Even without diving deep into technical diagrams, there are a few foundational principles that support proper IT/OT separation:

  • Network Segmentation Use VLANs or physically separate networks to isolate IT systems from OT systems. This isn’t just about labeling - it requires enforcement through firewalls or access control policies that prevent direct communication except for approved, logged pathways.

  • Data Aggregation Points Instead of pulling data directly from an OT device, route information through a central, read-only collection point. This limits exposure and ensures all data flows can be monitored and controlled.

  • Controlled Interfaces Any system that must connect both worlds (e.g., for monitoring or convenience) should be tightly managed. Use hardened jump boxes or gateways with strict access rules and full logging.

  • Logging and Alerting Monitor all access to OT networks. Alerts should be configured for unexpected traffic patterns, failed login attempts, or system changes. Visibility is critical - you can’t defend what you can’t see.

  • No Direct Internet on OT Systems Operational systems should never have unfiltered internet access. This removes the primary threat vector for most attacks.

The goal isn’t complexity, it’s control. Establish boundaries early, and enforce them consistently.

Final Thought

This isn’t just a cybersecurity issue. It’s a safety and operational continuity issue. Keeping IT and OT separate isn’t paranoia - it’s discipline. And everyone on board, from leadership to crew, needs to understand the difference.
